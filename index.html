Why Is Accessibility Important?

Inclusion matters. Tools like Bill-ID make everyday tasks like identifying cash simple and dignified for people with visual impairments. Our site uses accessibility-first design so everyone can navigate it with comfort.

In Brazil, where more than 6.5 million people live with some form of visual impairment, even recognizing money can become a barrier to independence. While the Brazilian Central Bank has included features like different sizes and tactile markings to distinguish banknotes, these features are often too subtle, difficult to memorize, or fade over time. As a result, many blind and visually impaired individuals still rely on others to confirm the value of their money, which can compromise their privacy, autonomy, and even safety.

The issue isn’t just about design—it’s about equity and dignity. In a country marked by deep social and economic inequalities, accessibility isn’t a luxury—it’s a necessity. People with disabilities, particularly in under-resourced communities, face multiple layers of exclusion. For them, accessible tools are essential to participate fully in society, manage their finances independently, and reduce the risk of being taken advantage of.

Brazil’s laws, including the Brazilian Inclusion Law (Lei Brasileira de Inclusão), aim to protect the rights of people with disabilities, but there is still a long way to go in making daily life genuinely accessible. That’s where inclusive technology comes in. Tools like Bill-ID don’t just bridge a technical gap—they reinforce people’s right to live with independence and respect.

By building accessibility into our design from the start, we’re not only helping individuals—we’re strengthening a more just and inclusive society. Because when accessibility is prioritized, everyone benefits.

How Bill-ID Works

The device detects the value of banknotes and announces it aloud. Here’s how it works:

  1. The system uses a sensor to detect the color of the banknote and processes the information.
  2. The microcontroller identifies the value of the bill.
  3. The audio player plays the sound file that announces the bill’s value (e.g., “Five Dollars”).
  4. The speaker outputs the audio, allowing the user to hear the value of the bill.
  5. The system runs on batteries, making it portable and easy to use.

We plan to add a headphone socket in the future, allowing users to hear the audio privately.

Instructions

  1. Press the button to turn the device on or off.
  2. Insert the banknote into the gap between the lens and the protective wall.
  3. Wait a few seconds for the system to recognize the value.
  4. Listen to the announcement through the speaker.
  5. Repeat with other banknotes if needed.
